## Notes on Preference-Based RL and Direct Policy Updates

![image](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/B1l7GFlGMg.png)

Something worth noting here: if we already have preference labels on the actions we take and on the possible final outcomes we can reach, then instead of training a reward function, we could jump straight to adjusting the probability of an action being taken based on how many successful or failed trajectories it generated. We basically cut out the reward model entirely — if we're already going through the trouble of collecting preference labels describing what a trajectory did, we can directly edit the probability of that action happening again (or not).

The key question is: to get the trajectory right, what action should be chosen at the next stage? This would really need to be run many times from each state, starting from one of those actions, to nudge action probabilities up or down appropriately. Alternatively, you treat it like REINFORCE: at the end, did the trajectory produce preferred outcomes? If so, nudge all the actions at each state that led to that outcome.

As it turns out, this is already a direction people have explored. Algorithms like **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)** allow you to edit a model's policy directly after preference labels are collected. DPO treats preference data (chosen vs. rejected responses) as a direct classification task to update policy weights, skipping the step of training a standalone reward model entirely.

And the multi-sample extension has also been done. One paper puts it directly:

> "However, current post-training methods such as reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) and direct alignment from preference methods (DAP) primarily utilize single-sample comparisons. These approaches often fail to capture critical characteristics such as generative diversity and bias, which are more accurately assessed through multiple samples..."

The paper proposes **Multi-sample DPO (mDPO)** and **Multi-sample IPO (mIPO)**, which compare groups of samples rather than just pairs. ([openreview.net](https://openreview.net/pdf/2a5308302da4b1a181a7b493aa2dd74232a23700.pdf))

A related idea — improving a model by training reward-function weights for a state-action pair using many trajectory outcomes simultaneously — also appears to have been explored recently (May 2026, in a diffusion-model setting): ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.26491v1))

And yet another paper takes a similar approach: ([ICML proceedings](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v202/zhu23f/zhu23f.pdf))

### Background on the Paper

This paper works within the standard MDP and RL setup: at every timestep, the agent receives a state and chooses an action; the environment returns a reward and transitions to a new state; the goal is to maximize cumulative discounted reward.

It builds on **preference-based RL**. Instead of directly providing reward values, an annotator compares two behaviors and indicates which is preferred. The Sutton & Barto framework is still respected — after all those state transitions, we end up at some preference result, which is used to infer reward.

Notably, they represent a trajectory segment using a single ending image. Based on that image, they update the reward model.

The reward-learning objective is defined over a dataset of preferences:

**D = {(σ₀, σ₁, y)}**

The reward model is trained to predict which segment should be preferred. Like most loss functions, it's ultimately doing the same thing at a high level: increase weights where needed, decrease them where not, so outputs match labels.

The policy πθ and reward function rψ are updated alternately — reward model updated from preferences, policy updated via RL on that learned reward. The underlying algorithm is **PEBBLE**.

### The Part That Doesn't Fully Make Sense

If preferences are already determining what is good and bad, and we're already training a reward model to capture that, why do we need a separate RL stage afterward? If the preferences already tell us what we want, why not skip the reward model and directly modify the policy based on aggregate preference information?

No reward model. No intermediate step. Just edit the policy directly based on whether actions led to preferred outcomes across trajectories.

As noted above, people have already explored that direction. But it's still worth flagging that this paper doesn't take that path.

---

### VLM-Generated Preferences

What's useful here is that preferences aren't generated by humans — they're generated from VLM context and literature knowledge. Human preference collection is expensive, so if a VLM can reliably produce useful preference labels, that's a meaningful practical improvement.

The paper compares two randomly selected trajectory endpoints, assigns preferences, and uses those to learn a reward model.

That said, the paper itself acknowledges a major limitation:

### Impact Statement

> Because the reward function is generated using a pretrained VLM, any biases present in the VLM can be inherited by the reward model and then by the learned policy. As a result, policies should be carefully evaluated before deployment in safety-critical settings.

In short: **don't use this for safety-critical systems without additional safeguards or human oversight.** If the VLM is the one deciding what outcomes are preferable, its biases flow directly into the policy.

### Open Questions

**Could there be a human in the loop after the VLM judge?** Could the final preference ranking be some combination of VLM judgment and human judgment? Nothing in the literature reviewed so far seems to address this directly.

**What if we compare trajectories that start from different actions?** Whatever produces a better preference, we nudge that action up. The multi-trajectory diagram below captures this idea:

![vla_multitrajectory_vlm_judging-1](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/Hk70wZXGMe.jpg)

![direct_policy_update_vs_reward_model-1](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/SkFbuWmGzx.jpg)

Supporting paper on why VLM-based preference labeling may be preferable to human majority opinion: ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.18290))

> "We might want our language model to be aware of a common misconception believed by 50% of people, but we certainly do not want the model to claim this misconception to be true in 50% of queries about it."

Selecting the model's desired behavior from its wide knowledge base is crucial to building AI systems that are safe, performant, and controllable. DPO-style methods offer a path to do that without RL.

The paper also mentions the **Plackett-Luce model**, though it's unclear from the text whether it's actually used in their experiments or just compared against.

The explanation of how the reward function weights get updated isn't spelled out clearly in the paper. Since reward requires state and action as inputs, the implication is that they reduce weights for actions that led to lower-preference outcomes and increase them for actions that led to higher-preference outcomes — but the implementation details aren't made explicit.

![image](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/HyOtzieGzg.png)

Using a VLM to generate preference labels is the obvious move. What the paper could have done better is go deeper on how they actually go in and edit the reward function weights for implementation purposes.
